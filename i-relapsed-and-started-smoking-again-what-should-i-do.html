I Relapsed and Started Smoking Again. What Should I Do?

Published April 24, 2026 · 5 min read

This phrase echoed in my head every time I made yet another promise to myself—over the last 10 years, out of a 25-year smoking history (not counting the past three).

I tried everything to ease the craving:

• Nicotine gum and patches

• Allen Carr’s books

• Visits to psychologists

• Arguments with friends

• Countless promises to myself: “If I smoke again, then…”

But before long, I’d find myself running out for cigarettes.

To say that every relapse felt like a personal betrayal would be an understatement. And if you really think about it, this whole cycle of tobacco use is so cruel that it forces a smoker to feel contempt—not for anyone else, but for themselves. In this story, I was always the villain. And that was the most painful, and fundamentally wrong, part of it.

I was the one making promises to others that I would quit.

I was the one chewing nicotine gum while smoking a cigarette.

I was the one looking in the mirror, seeing the culprit behind all this misery.

And that was exactly why I told everyone I’d smoke for the rest of my life.

But then, one day, I read an article that sparked the same kind of backlash my own writing later caused on Reddit.

When I finally read the actual scientific data, my first reaction wasn't just that quitting seemed pointless—it felt like a verdict. A life sentence.

Until…

Two or three months later, a strange little thought took shape in my head. It appeared in my mind like a magical fairy.

So, what’s the answer to the question at the top of this page?

“Exhale.”

Later, after I had already quit but before I even had a plan for this book, I realized how deeply ingrained the delusion is that a smoker must do something. It’s a genuinely unsettling belief, dear reader—one that leaves you speechless.

Smokers and everyone around them act as though accepting personal betrayal is just the norm. Yet the science—and frankly, any legitimate methodology—says the opposite: learning a new habit should be natural. Calm. Steady.

That single idea became the seed for my book, The Science of Indifference.

Want to quit while actually enjoying the process, and without feeling like you’re betraying yourself?
